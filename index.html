🌱 Recycling Behaviors Study

Hands-On Workshop vs. Awareness Video: A Randomized Controlled Trial

N = 100 Students | Ages 12-18 | March 2026

📊 Executive Summary

Key Finding

The hands-on recycling workshop increased observable recycling behavior by 24 percentage points (62.2% vs 38.2%, p = 0.0167) and worked exceptionally well for intrinsically motivated students (91.67% vs 51.85%, p = 0.0147), but did NOT improve knowledge, attitudes, or sorting accuracy.

🎯 Key Findings & Interpretation

What Worked ✅

  • Observable Behavior (H2b): Workshop increased recycling by 24 percentage points (62.2% vs 38.2%, p = 0.0167, V = 0.239)
  • Motivation Moderation (H4): Intrinsic students showed 39.82 pp gain in sustained participation (91.67% vs 51.85%, p = 0.0147)

What Didn't Work ❌

  • Sorting Accuracy (H1): No improvement (p = 0.7492, d = -0.136)
  • Self-Reported Frequency (H2a): No difference (p = 0.7528, r = 0.078)
  • Emotional Mediation (H3): Not operating (p = 0.964, ACME ≈ 0)
  • Knowledge Mediation (H5): Not operating (p = 0.920, ACME ≈ 0)
  • Attitude Change (H6): Identical scores (p = 0.5000, d = 0.00)

🔑 Key Takeaway

Hands-on workshops increase observable recycling behavior, especially for intrinsically motivated students, but don't improve knowledge, attitudes, or operate through hypothesized mediators. The mechanisms remain unclear and warrant further investigation.

🏛️ Public Policy Implications

1. Shift from Awareness to Action

  • Passive campaigns (videos) are not effective
  • Policies should prioritize hands-on, experiential programs
  • Focus on actual recycling practice rather than just information delivery

2. Target the Right Population

  • Strong effects only for intrinsically motivated individuals
  • Use segmentation strategies (schools, profiles, incentives)
  • Consider different approaches for extrinsically motivated populations

3. Rethink Behavior Change Strategies

  • Knowledge and attitudes did not drive behavior
  • Focus on behavioral engagement, social norms, and situational factors
  • Move beyond information campaigns and attitude change efforts

4. Design More Effective Interventions

  • Combine workshops with nudges or incentives
  • Encourage repetition and habit formation
  • Address inequality: workshops may widen gaps between motivated and unmotivated students
  • Develop complementary strategies for universal impact

⚠️ Limitations & Future Improvements

Limitations

  • Self-reported data: Potential bias in frequency and attitude measures
  • Short-term measurement: Only immediate post-intervention effects captured
  • Weak mediators: Possible measurement issues with emotional connection and knowledge retention
  • Sample limited: Students ages 12-18 only; generalizability concerns

Future Improvements

  • Better emotional measures with validated scales
  • Long-term tracking (3, 6, 12 months) to assess sustained behavior change
  • Larger sample size for more robust moderation/mediation analyses
  • Field experiments in real-world settings beyond schools
  • Test alternative mediators (social norms, peer influence, habits)

🔬 Statistical Methods

  • Group comparisons: Independent samples t-tests for continuous outcomes (H1, H6), Mann-Whitney U test for ordinal data (H2a), Chi-square test for categorical outcomes (H2b)
  • Mediation analyses (H3, H5): Baron-Kenny steps with bootstrapped confidence intervals (1000 simulations), tested Average Causal Mediation Effects (ACME)
  • Moderation analysis (H4): Logistic regression with interaction term (Intervention × Motivation), Likelihood ratio tests for model comparison
  • Effect sizes reported: Cohen's d, rank-biserial r, Cramér's V, and regression coefficients
  • Software: R (version 4.4.1) with packages: dplyr, ggplot2, coin, mediation, lmtest, sandwich
